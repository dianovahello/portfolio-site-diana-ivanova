Cookshop How should women design for men? Working on Cookshop, I got to conduct research to assess the unique needs of male home cooks (if any), and the extent to which existing products neglect to serve.Two interesting questions arose. The first relates to my own assumptions about recipe content online: does it really skew to a female default? Is this an obstacle for male cooks, and how? The second question felt simple but wasn't: what is "masculine" UI? The outcome was a responsive web app that helps an underserved group of home cooks express themselves reliably in the kitchen. The bonus is a brand that feels bold, exciting, and elegant.

My role: UXR and UI designer

Timeframe: 15 weeks

Tools: Spreadsheets, Markers, Figma

Understanding the context

I began by conducting secondary research into home cooking trends and outcomes, paying special attention to any differences by gender. I wanted to understand the unique context of home cooks who identify as male. I discovered a surprising trend: men are more likely to say they enjoy cooking, yet fewer meals are cooked by men.

  1. Men love to cook. Studies have shown that 82% of men enjoy home cooking, compared to just 75% of women.

  2. In spite of this, women cook much more than men in all countries. In 2020, only 1/3 of the meals cooked at home were prepared by men.

  3. Men are cooking more often. Still, young men continue to experience lower skills and confidence in cooking than young women at the stage of living on their own for the first time.

  4. Cooking at home is good for you. It leads to improved diet and health, greater happiness, and a longer life. Women and married men have so far benefited the most.

Understanding the landscape

I conducted market research to find out what solutions exist, and how they address the needs of male home cooks. I found that while a variety of recipe apps appear to be neutrally-targeted, their branding and tone may be biased towards a feminine assumption. Explicitly male-oriented material was overwhelmingly focused on fitness and health objectives. There was little to no recipe material addressing an audience of men who cook for pleasure.

Solution

Minimalist visual design with good contrast and clear hierarchy: for a lighter and more accessible product, the bells and whistles are invisible.

Interactions are intuitive: adherence to native platform design guidelines ensures interaction patterns are optimized and familiar.

Monetization

  1. Freemium model

    Core features are free while advanced features like custom reporting, expert financial advice, and some automations are available as a paid tier.

  2. Partnerships with financial institutions

    Partnerships for integrated services could provide revenue streams while offering partners the opportunity to offer a unique experience and secure a growing audience of (more on the target audience later).

  3. Additional services

    The brand identity is uniquely scalable, able to grow to offer coaching and other financial services for (more about them later).

Solution

The feature breakdown has something for everyone: new budgeters can enjoy core features to calm anxiety and build self-awareness. More confident budgeters can join a Premium tier for access to powerful automations, financial advice and more.

Let's talk about the elephant in the room.

Why build another budgeting app? In addition to asking the question I set out to understand what offerings exist on the market and assess potential opportunities for a new product. I cast a wide net, then zeroed in on Mint, RocketMoney, and Ally Bank’s “Spending Buckets” feature for a deeper look.

What are competitors doing right?

Dynamic data visualizations help users see their status.

Automatic income and expense tracking is convenient.

Data ‐driven insights are informative.

Show me the bad reviews.

Too many features can be confusing.

Steep learning curves can be discouraging.

Too much information can be overwhelming.

What are competitors missing?

By this time, I had used four apps to plan my September budget, read about eight more, and watched about 4 hours’ worth of in-depth video reviews. As I tallied pros and cons, a picture started to form. I began to suspect that an important person was slipping through the cracks.

They failed to support people with multiple income streams, no income, or negative income.

They relied on gamification and aggressive alerts, which can increase financial anxiety.

They offered excessive automation features, which can increase feelings of control loss.

Theme 1: "Budgeting apps think I live in the 50’s."

While budgeting tools continue to ask what our income is (as if there is a simple answer), the workforce is changing. Makers, nomads, side-hustlers, social media creators, gig workers and students have multiple streams of unpredictable or even negative income. Edge cases are quickly becoming the norm: 1/3 of America is freelancing today, but in just 3 years that figure will go over 1/2!

"I needed another app just to calculate what to put into this app. What's up with that?!"

Sources: Freelancers Union, Statista, Google Trends.

Solution

Multiple income streams are a matter of course. Go large, keep steady or take a break: no judgment ever.

Theme 2: "Budgeting apps are intimidating."

"Why does this look like the Matrix?," offered one interview participant. "If I was a math genius I wouldn’t need this app in the first place."

Up to 30% of people report moderate to high levels of math anxiety. Research shows that some level of math anxiety affects up to 93% of US adults! By failing to address this, budgeting apps are failing a huge swath of the population. (New York Times, Luttenberger et al).

Bias warning: a popular fallacy states "creative people are not math people" because of "the left and right side of the brain." This is a false concept that emerged during interviews: creative hobbyists and professionals shared they felt intimidated by charts, graphs, and spreadsheets.

Creatives are far more likely to freelance, engage in gig work, or have a side hustle like selling handmade items. 55% of Entertainment workers freelance, while in Arts and Design the figure jumps to 75% (Zippia). This means that people affected by the false right brain/left brain dichotomy are also more likely to have complex income models.

Solution

A simple ritual to calm anxiety: Plan, Track, Reflect, repeat. Choose to be guided or make your own path.

Theme 3: "Budgeting apps don’t help me grow."

Users shared a desire to understand spending and money management and improve their habits. Those who tried a budgeting app and deleted it, did so because it failed to provide lasting change. Every notification was an unwanted reminder that they are failing to stay within budget.

"I felt like Pavlov's dog," signed one interview participant."I don’t want to just connect my bank and get a bunch of stats - I wanted to do better."

Solution

Build self-awareness by assigning each of your expenses to a mindful category: Need, Want, Culture, or the Unexpected.

Grow accountability by planning your own time to check in: no automations, and no alarms unless you set one.

Key learnings

  1. Native app design.

    To ensure an intuitive user experience, I had to strike a balance between creativity and adhering to platform conventions. I came away with deep knowledge of iOS and Android design guidelines. I also learned how constraints can boost creativity and drive innovation.

  2. Cultural insights.

    The Kakeibo-inspired mindfulness integration met with a positive response from users during testing. This showed me that cross-cultural insights can offer unique solutions that work.
