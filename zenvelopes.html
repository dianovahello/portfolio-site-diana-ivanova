Defining the problem

Back to table of contents

We live at a time of change. As more individuals shift to freelance work, even full-time employees are finding ways to monetize their passions. The freedom of this can feel amazing, but it comes at the cost of complicating our monthly budget. Increasingly we need to manage multiple income streams, big fluctuations month to month, optional expenses and more. Who is most affected, and do existing apps help?

Key target users

Makers, nomads, side-hustlers, social media creators, and freelancers. Anyone with unpredictable or sometimes negative income, such as a student. They may experience financial anxiety, and may describe themselves as “not a math person”.

Key competitors

I explored Rocket Money, Mint, and the Spending Buckets feature offered by Ally Bank. I found that while they offer powerful features like dynamic data visualization, automatic expense tracking, alerts, and insights, they suffer from steep learning curves and too many features. Furthermore, they do not address financial anxiety or offer a path to a healthier and more thoughtful relationship with money.

Enter kakeibo

Back to table of contents

Kakeibo is a budgeting system from medieval Japan. It's well adapted to help the contemporary anxious budgeter because it's minimalist and growth-minded in its approach. A Kakeibo practitioner divides their expenses into four categories: needs, wants, culture, and the unexpected. The user is not overwhelmed by tracking 50 envelopes with labels like “Netflix” or “Car insurance”. They are empowered to make mindful decisions about spending.

user flow

Back to table of contents

I stuctured the app along three major flows. The Plan flow guides the user through inputting their projected income and expenses, adding an optional savings goal, and scheduling a day to track and reflect. Any flow is accessible at any time from the homepage. Reminders to track and reflect are set by the user themselves.

iOS vs Android

Back to table of contents

Zenvelopes is a native app built for iOS and Android. To ensure seamless integration with each OS's native features and interactions, as well as a smooth native experience, I made a deep study of Human Interface and Material Design guidelines, then worked side-by-side on every screen starting at the mid-fidelity level.

touch, sounds, and gestures

Back to table of contents

I leveraged platform-specific gestures, sound cues, and subtle haptics to improve usability, reinforce the native feel of the app, and create a reassuring sense of responsiveness. Where appropriate, I implemented a soothing “paper theme” consistent with the ink-on-parchment look of the UI.

visual direction

Back to table of contents

The overall visual direction for this app is minimalist, typography-based, and reinforces a Zen theme. I used native fonts and icons to ensure a smooth experience. The color palette is effectively monochromatic. Parchment or sand texture is applied to some screens, reinforcing a theme of mindfulness and calm.

In keeping with this system, the logo is a minimal set of two ink strokes that create an “equal” sign, signifying balance. Their positions relative to each other evidence the letter “Z”.

Testing and revisions

Back to table of contents

I built two high-fidelity prototypes with animated interactions, and performed user testing with iOS and Android users. I wanted to assess how easily users can follow the planning flow, how confident they feel while planning their budget, and to what extent they find the interactions to be natural and familiar. With user feedback, I applied several revisions. I reduced the number of screens, added informative tooltips and slideouts.
